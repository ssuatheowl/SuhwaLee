SUMMARY

Carepay’s Agent app was built to facilitate healthcare agents to enroll people to state’s and private healthcare program to provide them with healthcare benefits. Despite 1000 agents using the app daily, the app seemed to have very poor usability along with its rough look. To assess the status quo and to convince the business to agree to the major redesign, we used various usability assessment methodologies. We also established a user research ritual that can be applied to any future projects.

CHALLENGE

During the peak days, almost 1000 agents per day in Kenya and Nigeria use the Carepay Agent app to enroll new beneficiaries across the country, reaching out to the poor living in remote villages.

While the app had been up and running for 2 years, no feedback had been gathered, and no UX improvements had been made. The app looked rough and difficult to use.

Our design team wanted to turn this to an opportunity to advocate human-centered design by starting user research. I wanted to listen to the end-user’s feedback and made design decisions based on that. As a team, we also wanted to establish a user research ritual that we can continue following in the future.

1

How do we pitch to the business to support us to start any form of usability testing while it had never been done before?

2

How might we effectively facilitate a remote usability test for a mobile application, while looking at both of the participant and the app?

3

How might we effectively record the remote usability test session for our research repository?

THE BEGINNING
— Usability-evaluation method by usability experts

We wanted to demonstrate the poor usability of our product and highlight the value of usability assessment. We had to quantify the usability without breaking the bank. Our MVP approach was to use PURE (Pragmatic Usability Rating by Experts) method. A team of 4 designers rated all major features of the app. It was not only a very cost-effective approach, it also had much more credibility than me calling for a bad usability.



Starting small but surely
— Sending out simple surveys to collect quantified results

After establishing that our app wasn’t anywhere where we wanted to be, we gained more traction to validate the bad usability with the end-users. This time, the System Usability Scale (SUS) was used to evaluate the product usability quantitatively. We sent out a spreadsheet to 10 of our users.

We also assessed customer satisfaction by asking our users to rate the app on a scale of 1 to 5.







As expected, the result did not look any good.





“OK, we get it.
The usability is bad.
What do we do now?”



We finally got our chance to conduct a user research to dive into all the usability problems and UX issues with our end-users.

DESIGN

Based on the learnings of the user research, I made major design changes to improve the usability and user experience of the app.

To help the less tech-savvy agents using the app, brief onboarding tutorials were added.

The home page was restructured to show the statuses of registrations at glance.

Throughout the registration process, clear hint texts, error and confirmation messages were added to reduce human errors when filling out a lengthy registration form for a health scheme.

CREATING A RITUAL

To promote the ritual of objectively measuring usability, we created a page to show the results at glance. We aimed to continue filling the page in with research results for all our products.

IMPACT

+0.54

The CSAT score showed a 12.5% increase after the redesign.
The number of support calls for using the app was reduced.
The training time and budget for the agents have been reduced.
